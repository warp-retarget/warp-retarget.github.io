WARP

Whole-Body Retargeting for Learning from Offline Human Demonstrations

Anonymous project page for review

TL;DR: WARP converts offline human demonstrations into precise, consistent whole-body robot actions in closed form — enabling open-loop replay and policy learning for whole-body mobile manipulation, with no teleoperation in the loop.

Abstract

Direct transfer from human demonstration to learnable robot action is a crucial step towards scalable whole-body mobile manipulation. While human data scales better than mobile teleoperation, it requires overcoming significant embodiment gaps: existing retargeting methods yield imprecise or inconsistent solutions, causing action multi-modality that prevents supervised policies from reliably converging.

We present Whole-body-Aware Retargeting from human Pose (WARP), an offline pipeline that explicitly models embodiment differences to extract precise, unique whole-body actions. WARP leverages a closed-form Shoulder–Elbow–Wrist (SEW) geometric solver for exact end-effector tracking while preserving whole-body structural intent. Paired with lazy mobile-base control, it extracts accurate, consistent robot trajectories.

Evaluations show WARP provides highly reliable data for open-loop real-world replay. To our knowledge, WARP is the first framework to achieve zero-shot whole-body mobile manipulation directly from offline human demonstrations, eliminating the need for human-in-the-loop teleoperation action data.

Why Offline Whole-Body Retargeting?

During teleoperation, a human operator closes the loop in real time — if the robot wrist drifts or picks a bad IK branch, the operator compensates. Offline, there is no such correction: the retargeted trajectory is the supervision. This makes two failure modes of existing retargeters fatal:

  • Imprecision. Weighted objectives trade end-effector accuracy against whole-body similarity; either way, retargeting error becomes supervision error.
  • Inconsistency. Humanoids are highly redundant, so nearly identical human poses can map to different robot configurations depending on solver initialization — pairing similar observations with divergent actions and impeding policy learning.

WARP is precise, consistent, and smooth. (a) WARP matches both the end-effector constraint and the human body pose; baselines satisfy only one. (b) Under perturbed initial guesses, optimization-based MINK is inconsistent, while WARP's closed-form solution is identical every time. (c) A responsive upper body compensates for the low-frequency lazy base, smoothing the base trajectory.

Method

WARP is a geometry-based retargeter paired with a policy trained on its output. Its core, c-SEW, solves retargeting in closed form over the Shoulder–Elbow–Wrist (SEW) representation: instead of optimizing in redundant joint space, it constructs a target robot skeleton with the palm matched as a hard constraint, then recovers joint angles analytically — no iterative solver at any stage.

Offline retargeting with WARP. (a) Adaptive offset finds the optimal robot torso placement. (b) Aligning the robot palm to the human palm determines the robot wrist. (c) Exact palm alignment leaves the elbow underconstrained. (d) The elbow nullspace is resolved by transferring the human elbow angle, yielding the solved robot SEW skeleton.

  • Adaptive offset. Shift the robot upper-body origin so the two-arm palm centroids coincide with the human's, absorbing link-length differences in closed form.
  • Per-arm palm alignment. Hand orientation transfers directly; the robot wrist follows analytically from the palm target; the elbow is placed on the human's SEW half-plane, with at most one valid configuration per arm.
  • Lazy mobile-base tracking. The 6-DoF torso absorbs small upper-body adjustments while the base moves only for genuine relocation — stable manipulation and smooth base motion at once.
  • Hierarchy-masked flow policy. A single flow-matching head with block-causal attention over base ≼ torso ≼ arm ≼ hand, so proximal motion is never explained by distal features, at unchanged inference cost.

The result is a unique solution for any non-degenerate pose, exact palm matching by construction, preserved whole-body similarity, and microsecond solve times with no per-robot rescaling or calibration.

Data & platform. Demonstrations are collected with a single Meta Quest headset — whole-body motion with full hand articulation at 60 Hz, no mocap rig or robot in the loop. We deploy the RB-Y1 humanoid (holonomic base, 6-DoF torso, two 7-DoF arms, 12-DoF five-fingered XHands) under joint-impedance control at 100 Hz.

Results

We test three hypotheses: H1 — WARP produces higher-quality whole-body motion than end-effector-only and generic retargeting baselines; H2 — these motion properties translate into measurably better policy learning; H3 — for tasks that depend on torso, elbow, or base placement, end-effector-only retargeting is fundamentally insufficient.

Simulation: Downstream Policy Learning (H2)

We retarget 200 DexMimicGen demonstrations per task (GR1 → RB-Y1 with dexterous hands) using WARP and MINK, then train an identical policy on each. Replay rates are comparable — but policies trained on WARP data succeed 12% more on average, with the largest gap on fine-grained manipulation (coffee). Retargeting nuances invisible at replay become decisive downstream. WARP also transfers the same motion across robot embodiments (above).

Real-World Evaluation (H2, H3)

We collect 50 human demonstrations per task across four real-world tasks: pick-up-laundry (bimanual wrist precision), push-cart (base–arm coordination under contact), rotate-box (torso–arm coordination), and fridge-door-closing — pushing the door shut with the elbow, a contact no end-effector-only formulation can even express.

WARP outperforms MINK across all four tasks (10 trials each). It keeps palm contact and recoverable posture where MINK drives the torso into extreme or colliding configurations, replays the elbow-contact fridge task in 90% of trials, and trains stronger policies even at comparable replay rates (65% vs. 40% on rotate-box) — motion quality, not replay completion, governs downstream success.

Limitations

Our current policy training lacks image observations, which limits the range of tasks we can attempt. We plan to investigate visual-motor policy learning next, and how WARP-retargeted offline human data can support it.
